## Problem 22  <!-- [ver:A][p:22] -->

Given $Q_D = 24 - 2P$, $Q_S = 12 + 2P$.

### 22(a) Graphing Supply and Demand Curves  <!-- [ver:A][p:22][a] -->

Invert the demand and supply equations (that is, write in "$P=$" form) and then graph the lines. It is clear from the "$P=$" form what the price intercept is.

Demand (inverse demand):
$$
Q_D = 24 - 2P \;\Rightarrow\; 2P = 24 - Q \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{P = 12 - \tfrac{1}{2}Q}.
$$
So the demand price intercept is $\boxed{12}$.

Supply (inverse supply):
$$
Q_S = 12 + 2P \;\Rightarrow\; 2P = Q - 12 \;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{P = -6 + \tfrac{1}{2}Q}.
$$
So the supply price intercept is $\boxed{-6}$.

### 22(b) Equilibrium  <!-- [ver:A][p:22][b][key:eq] -->

Set the demand equation equal to the supply equation, and solve for $P^*$:
$$
24 - 2P = 12 + 2P
\;\Rightarrow\; 12 = 4P
\;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{P^* = 3}.
$$
Then $Q^* = 24 - 2(3) = \boxed{18}$.

### 22(c) $Q_t$ with tax $t=2$ on sellers  <!-- [ver:A][p:22][c][key:tax] -->

To analyze imposing a tax on sellers, shift the inverse supply curve up by the amount of the tax, then use that shifted supply curve to find $Q_t$.

With a per-unit tax $t=2$ on sellers, the inverse supply curve shifts up by 2:
$$
P = \Bigl(-6 + \tfrac{1}{2}Q\Bigr) + 2 = -4 + \tfrac{1}{2}Q.
$$

Set equal to inverse demand from part (a):
$$
12 - \tfrac{1}{2}Q = -4 + \tfrac{1}{2}Q
\;\Rightarrow\; 16 = Q
\;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{Q_t = 16}.
$$

### 22(d) Buyers’ price  <!-- [ver:A][p:22][d] -->

To find the buyers' price, plug $Q_t$ into the original demand function to find the $P_b$ at which they demand $Q_t$.

Using $Q_D = 24 - 2P_b$ and $Q_t=16$:
$$
16 = 24 - 2P_b
\;\Rightarrow\; 2P_b = 8
\;\Rightarrow\; \boxed{P_b = 4}.
$$

### 22(e) Incidence of tax <!-- [ver:A][p:22][e][key:incidence] -->

To determine incidence, compare the change in buyers’ price and sellers’ price relative to the pre-tax equilibrium.

Sellers receive $P_s = P_b - t = 4 - 2 = \boxed{2}$.

Before the tax, $P^* = 3$. After the tax, buyers pay $4$ (up $+1$) and sellers receive $2$ (down $-1$). Thus: **Tax burden split evenly by buyers and sellers.**

## Problem 23  <!-- [ver:A][p:23] -->

### 23(a) Output at $P=4$  <!-- [ver:A][p:23][a] -->

At price $P=4$, each firm produces up to the quantity where marginal cost is weakly below price.

Firm A supplies $\boxed{4}$ units (since MC $1,2,3,4 \le 4$).

Firm B supplies $\boxed{2}$ units (since MC $2,4 \le 4$ and the next unit has MC $6>4$).

### 23(b) Cost-minimizing allocation for total $Q=6$  <!-- [ver:A][p:23][b][key:alloc] -->

To minimize total cost, allocate production to the lowest marginal cost units across both firms.

The 6 lowest marginal costs are:  
$A: 1,2,3,4$ and $B: 2,4$.

Thus the cost-minimizing allocation is $\boxed{Q_A=4}$ and $\boxed{Q_B=2}$. Note that at those quantities, $MC_A = MC_B$.
